Week Four

Prompt: Considering these articles, make a list of 5 things to keep you going when you feel resistance or discomfort.

Response: Five things to keep me going when I'm feeling resistance or discomfort would have to be:

1. to remember that I cannot grow without stepping into an area that is beyond my comfort zone, this especially applies to learning something new and developing a skill. When I was learning to draw, I was very discouraged in the beginning with the art work I created. I thought it was ugly and childish looking and it made me want to give up, until I changed my outlook and thought, "I don't care anymore, I want to draw and I will just keep drawing ugly people and animals and I am fine with drawing these ugly people and animals for the rest of my days." And that really ended up taking the pressure off and getting rid of the expectation that I should be drawing anything else. I slowly got better at drawing, each time I learned something new from a drawing and I was encouraged when I looked at my drawings from the beginning compared to the ones I was producing later on. I now realize much later that ugly people and animals are still art and that art is subjective and they can bring as much joy as naturalistic or "realistic" looking art. I actually prefer the ugly drawings now lol.


2. to remember that when learning new things, it is just part of the process to feel embarrased and incompetent. Nobody starts out an expert. You've got to be willing to look like a fool to get places. And you'll be happy that you did.


3. to be kind and patient with myself and give myself extra love, care and understanding. And like the articles mentioned, to know that it takes bravery to step out of a place of comfort into the unknown and take a shot in the dark. It's easy and safe to be a boat tied to a dock, but the real adventure is in the open, scary, magnificent seas.


4. to be aware of my natural inclination towards resistance and procrastination when faced with doing something that is out of my comfort zone. It's easy for me to spiral into a pattern of negative self talk and tell myself that I can't do something or I'll never be good at it, and to procrastinate because of these limiting beliefs but awareness of them gives me some power back and can help me to tell that roommate in my head, "thanks nancy for your concern, but I'm ok and I'm gonna go code now because I'm an intelligent, funny and cool human and I can do this, some way, somehow."


5. the knowledge that I'm not alone and that many other people have gone through the same feelings in order to expand their worlds in a great and empowering way.

Week Eight

Prompt: Read this article on mobile-first design and share your thoughts on mobile-first vs desktop first.

Response: I think that it makes sense to start from a “mobile first” or progressive advancement approach. Like the article mentioned, there are now people spending more time online with the use of their mobile phones than on desktops. There are also more mobile phones being bought than computers/ laptops. It is more accessible for people to quickly visit websites on their phones on the go, wherever they may be. I think it makes sense especially compared to designing for desktop first or from a graceful degradation approach because like the article mentioned, you end up making an awesome website and then as a sort of afterthought- or so it may appear- you design a mobile version that ends up being a watered-down version of the awesome desktop version. Designing from a mobile first approach allows you to streamline your design and really get concise and neat designs with exactly what you need to show off a product and nothing more or less. You can then add some features and interactions that the desktop version will allow without having the mobile version of your site suffer. And this is important because there may be a lot more people seeing the mobile version than the desktop version.

Prompt: This one's about "why." Read this article (try to pretend it's not a cheezy article about business success). Spend some time thinking about why you are pursuing a career in design. Really dig down, do the 7 "whys" tool in the article. OR If that's too challenging right now, consider people in your life or people who've inspired you. Think about what drives them, what their "why" is. Why did they work so hard to do XYZ, was it from past experiences they had, was it because they saw a need they were called to fill? What's their deeper "why?" Ask them if you know them, or look up some biographical information and try to figure it out. Write about either you or your inspiration.

Response: My why for being in college started out being something I did because I felt an expectation from others (my family mostly). I didn’t love high school so I was apprehensive about going back to an environment that didn’t make me feel good. I started at pcc when I was nineteen and took a variety of part time classes for a long time not knowing what I wanted to do. I was happy to find out that pcc was nothing like high school and I was actually sad to leave when I transferred to psu. In 2016 I took a drawing class and rediscovered how much I love drawing and creating. I had spent a lot of time as a child drawing and coloring but art disappeared from my life as I became a teenager. I’ve been drawing pretty consistently since then. There will be a few months here and there when I don’t draw or paint because I’m consumed with responsibilities but I never go too long before I return to it. I decided I wanted to do graphic design because at first, it felt like the most reasonable choice where I could make a living and still have some sense of art at the center of my life. During the journey, I’ve found I love telling stories, designing, and creating illustrations and other elements to aid in the final result. And coding has been a new joy and utter frustration but also sort of like an itch I have to scratch because nothing feels better than refreshing a webpage to see that whatever thing you’ve been trying to make work for an hour is finally where it’s supposed to be.
